Why CLADIS

Seven structural failure points found in the manual rotation sheet — Thursday, June 25/26

This analysis is based on a single day's rotation sheet — IR, Thursday 6/25/26. The sheet shows at least 7 structural failure points that are built into the manual process — not caused by human error, but by the design of the system itself. Each one compounds the others.

Error 1 — Ghost rotation slots
Lesly G (TX) is highlighted in teal and marked TX on the sheet. She appears in the PD rotation 4+ times but every row is blank or wavy — no claim assigned. The rotation advances past her anyway, pushing load onto the next adjuster in line.
Sheet evidence: 4 Lesly G rows with no claim numbers, all cycling the queue forward
Error 2 — Re-entry drift
Bottom right of the sheet: "Sandra in rotation 6/19 – 6/26." Sandra was out of rotation for a period and returned. There is no correction mechanism. Every claim she missed is permanently credited to whoever covered her slot. This is the root cause of the 15–20 variance Josh sees monthly.
Sheet evidence: handwritten note, no adjustment column, no retrospective count correction
Error 3 — Splits counted as one
A split claim assigns work to a PD adjuster AND a BI adjuster. The paper log shows one row. Both adjusters absorb a full claim of work, but the tally sees one. Michelle could have 7 splits — that's 14 units of real work — and look lighter on paper than Sandra's 12 new claims.
Sheet evidence: PD Split and BI Split are independent columns with no linked logic
Error 4 — Weight is invisible
A fatality and a fender-bender both count as 1. Ron's 12 claims could be all fatalities while Sandra's 18 are all minor. The count-based system makes Ron look light. There is no column for complexity, severity, or time cost on the manual sheet.
Sheet evidence: no severity or weight column anywhere on the form
Error 5 — Clerk data is a dead end
The Clerk column (AS, JF, RP, EP, IR, LL, CL) captures who in FNOL took the call. But this data lives only on this sheet, in this week's folder, in handwriting. It cannot be searched. There is no audit trail from adjuster back to intake clerk. One mis-initial and the chain breaks.
Sheet evidence: clerk initials on every row, no digital record, no searchable index
Error 6 — Cut-off has no version control
Top of sheet: "Cut off SA @ 1pm." The cut-off adjuster changes daily and is handwritten. If someone pulls yesterday's sheet by mistake, every assignment starts from the wrong rotation position. There is no timestamp, no confirmation, no validation.
Sheet evidence: "Cut off SA @ 1pm" at top — handwritten, not time-stamped, no verification
Error 7 — Totals never filled in
The bottom of the sheet has PD–, BI–, PERU–, SNAP–, TOTAL– with nothing filled in. Josh has no daily number from this sheet. He reconstructs monthly variance from incomplete, partial records — meaning his variance number is probably understated.
Sheet evidence: all total fields blank at bottom of June 25/26 sheet
The CLADIS response
CLADIS eliminates all 7 errors structurally. Rotation is queue-based with skip logic for PTO. Splits link PD and BI assignments in one record. Weight tags are built into intake. Clerk initials are captured digitally and searchable. Cut-off is automatic. Totals compute live. No sheet required.
The system isn't broken because people make mistakes. It's broken because the process has no memory, no weight, and no feedback loop. CLADIS gives it all three.
